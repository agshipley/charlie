## Core Thesis

The entertainment industry is undergoing a structural reorganization driven by the convergence of three forces:

1. **Supply-side exhaustion.** Traditional IP pipelines (books, comics, spec scripts) are showing signs of saturation — inflated option prices, declining hit rates, and insufficient volume to meet streaming-era demand.

2. **Demand-side migration.** Audiences have migrated from scripted content to creator-driven platforms, pulled not primarily by content preference but by radically superior algorithmic discovery infrastructure. YouTube and TikTok match audiences to content individually; streamers broadcast to everyone and hope for the best.

3. **The discovery bridge.** Creator-branded content may be the only reliable mechanism for bringing migrated audiences back into scripted consumption — not because the IP is better, but because the creator's audience relationship functions as a substitute for the algorithmic discovery that streamers lack.

The restructuring is not optional. It is being forced by the simultaneous depletion of traditional IP sources and the migration of audiences to platforms with fundamentally different content economics.

## Analytical Framework

### The Full IP Landscape

The thesis tracks two parallel systems and their interaction.

#### Traditional/Institutional IP (evaluate for saturation)

For each category, the research question is: **Is this pipeline producing enough viable material to meet current demand, and what is the evidence for saturation or exhaustion?**

- **Books/publishing** — The oldest pipeline. Option economics, depth of bench after decades of mining, inflation of acquisition prices, declining conversion rate from option to production.
- **Comic books** — Marvel/DC fatigue evidence. Independent comics and manga as potentially undertapped. Work-for-hire ownership structures vs. creator-owned.
- **Theater/Broadway** — Adaptation both directions. Hamilton model. Stage-to-screen and screen-to-stage economics.
- **Journalism/magazine articles** — True story pipeline. Evolution from magazine features to podcast-driven true crime.
- **Life rights/true crime** — Heavily mined, but does the podcast layer give it new life or just repackage the same material?
- **Video games** — Last of Us, Fallout, Mario. Treat as one category. Pipeline depth, adaptation success rate, audience crossover evidence.
- **Songs/jukebox model** — Bohemian Rhapsody, Rocketman, now Piña Colada in development. Early stage, largely untapped catalog. Different proof-of-concept model (cultural recognition rather than narrative structure).
- **Board games/toys** — Barbie proved the model. Replicable or one-off?

#### Creator-Driven IP (evaluate for growth trajectory)

For each category, the research question is: **What is the current volume of development activity, what is the conversion rate from option to production, and what structural advantages does this IP have over traditional sources?**

- **Podcasts/audio** — Most mature. Audiochuck, Wondery, Spotify, iHeartMedia, Gimlet, QCode. Track deal flow, adaptation success rates, evolving ownership structures.
- **YouTube/long-form creator content** — MrBeast-to-Netflix, creator-to-studio deals. Track which creators have crossed over and with what results.
- **Short-form/TikTok** — Nascent but accelerating. Regulatory uncertainty as a complication.
- **Newsletters/Substack** — Early stage. The Optionist model, Free Press/Paramount, Puck ecosystem.
- **Livestreaming/gaming creators** — Crossover plays. Markiplier, Kai Cenat, the gaming-to-entertainment pipeline.

#### The Interaction Between Systems

Traditional pipeline exhaustion creates demand pressure that pushes studios toward creator-driven IP. Research should track evidence of this causal relationship, not just the parallel trends.

### Audience Migration Analysis

#### Why audiences are leaving scripted content

The research should distinguish between content-driven and infrastructure-driven causes:

- **Algorithmic discovery gap.** YouTube, TikTok, and Spotify recommendation engines match audiences to content individually and continuously. Streamers use blunt-instrument category browsing and promotional placement. Audiences are being *pulled* to creator platforms by superior discovery, not necessarily *pushed* from scripted content by quality decline.
- **Parasocial intimacy.** Creator audiences feel they know the host. This relationship doesn't exist with scripted content. The adaptation challenge is preserving this quality in translation.
- **Discovery trust.** Audiences trust creator recommendations over institutional marketing. The creator brand functions as a targeting mechanism that streamers cannot replicate internally.
- **On-demand depth vs. scheduled breadth.** Creator platforms enable niche depth. Scripted TV historically offered genre breadth. Migrated audiences resist returning to breadth.
- **Format flexibility.** Podcast/YouTube audiences consume across contexts (commuting, exercising). Scripted content is less context-flexible.
- **Authenticity vs. production polish.** Some migration is a rejection of overproduced content in favor of perceived authenticity. There may be a ceiling on how "produced" a creator-adapted show can feel before losing the quality that attracted the audience.

#### Target demo analysis by platform

Track which streamer is chasing which demographic gap, because this determines who buys which creator-driven IP:

- **Netflix** ad-tier: needs 18-34 multicultural volume viewers (currently on YouTube/TikTok)
- **Amazon/Prime Video**: 30-50 female household decision-makers (Wondery true crime alignment)
- **Apple TV+**: affluent, educated 25-55 prestige seekers (creator IP only when it carries cachet)
- **Hulu/Disney+**: 18-34 corridor aging out of animated/Marvel pipeline
- **Peacock**: broadcast-adjacent 35-55 female-skewing (strongest natural audience for podcast-to-scripted)
- **YouTube (as streamer)**: already owns 18-34, optimizing for longer sessions on CTV

#### Conversion and retention

- Which demos convert from creator platform to scripted viewing?
- Do creator-branded shows have different retention curves than traditionally marketed shows?
- Does the creator brand function as ongoing retention (podcast keeps reinforcing) or one-time acquisition?
- True crime podcast audiences (female, 25-45) may be the highest-conversion opportunity for scripted drama — test this hypothesis.

### Creator-Driven Company Development Functions

#### The institutional gap

The existing literature says almost nothing about what happens when a creator-led company needs to professionalize its development function. The thesis should investigate:

- **What does the development executive's job look like at Audiochuck vs. at a traditional studio?** Different IP origination, different creator relationships, different brand protection requirements.
- **IP and brand protection.** Creator-driven companies are incentivized to protect IP and brand differently than traditional licensors. The creator IS the brand — a bad adaptation doesn't just hurt one show, it damages the entire company's value proposition, podcast downloads, talent recruitment, and advertising revenue across the portfolio.
- **Valuation differences.** Creator-driven IP must be valued on audience relationship depth (engagement, loyalty, conversion rates), not comparable sales. No established framework exists for this.
- **Creative control structures.** Creator-led companies have existential reasons to insist on creative approval. The development exec must navigate between institutional production standards and creator brand integrity.
- **The "proof of concept" model.** Podcast audiences function as market validation before development spend. This inverts traditional development risk.

#### Development executive tracking

Build a dataset of who has been hired into development roles at creator-native companies:

- **Aaron Hart** — Head of TV and Film, Wondery. Track record: Dirty John, Dr. Death, Shrink Next Door, Joe vs. Carole, WeCrashed. Background, methodology, hit rate.
- **Matt Shanfield** — Audiochuck, hired March 2026 from Sony nonfiction. No track record yet; hiring itself is the data point.
- **Head of Scripted TV, Audiochuck** — role Liz is being considered for. Track the hire and mandate.
- **Jordan Moblo** — EVP Creative Acquisitions & IP Management, Universal Studio Group / UCP Audio.
- **Wondery restructuring (August 2025)** — who stayed, left, was brought in during the pivot to creator-led and video-focused.
- **Fox Entertainment / Meet Cute acquisition** — who runs development there?
- **Any new hires at Spotify Studios, iHeartMedia, or other audio companies expanding into filmed content.**

For each person: background, where they came from, what they've produced, conversion rate from development to production, critical/audience reception of produced content.

### The Discovery Bridge Argument

This should be a central thesis pillar:

**Creator-branded content functions as the only reliable discovery mechanism for bringing algorithmically-sorted audiences back into scripted consumption.** The value of the creator brand isn't the IP itself — it's the *targeting* that the creator brand provides for free that would cost the streamer millions in marketing and that its own recommendation infrastructure cannot replicate.

Research should investigate:
- Evidence that streamer recommendation engines are inadequate for matching audiences to scripted content
- Evidence that creator audiences respond to cross-format recommendations from trusted creator brands
- Whether Amazon is using Wondery audience data to improve Prime Video recommendations (and if not, why not)
- The economics of creator-driven audience acquisition vs. traditional marketing spend
- Whether the feedback loop exists: creator data → better recommendations → better retention

## Source Hierarchy

### Tier 1: Structural Analysts (highest priority, cite frequently)
- **Matthew Ball** — streaming economics, content valuation frameworks, value chain analysis. His essays on Netflix economics and the unbundling/rebundling of entertainment are foundational.
- **Richard Rushfield / The Ankler** — structural critique of the studio development model, industry dysfunction analysis.
- **Matthew Belloni / Puck** — deal-level intelligence, Hollywood power dynamics, institutional decision-making.

### Tier 2: Data Platforms (essential for empirical grounding)
- **Parrot Analytics** — cross-platform audience demand measurement. Closest thing to unified audience data.
- **Nielsen** — streaming share of TV, viewership data, demographic breakdowns.
- **Edison Research** — podcast audience demographics and behavior. Annual Infinite Dial study is the gold standard.
- **Luminate** — music and entertainment data.

### Tier 3: Academic Research
- **Cunningham & Craig** — *Social Media Entertainment* (NYU Press, 2019). Foundational on the NoCal/SoCal clash and creator ecosystem institutionalization.
- **Hesmondhalgh** — critical perspective on whether digital platforms have actually democratized media industries.
- **Duffy** — labor economics of creator work, *(Not) Getting Paid to Do What You Love* (Yale, 2017).
- **Kim (2012)** — institutionalization of YouTube.
- **Vonderau (2016)** — MCN economics and the "video bubble."
- **Florida (2022)** — *Rise of the Creator Economy* report.
- **MidiaResearch (2025)** — creator franchise framework, audience ownership vs. reach.
- **WIPO (2024)** — AI and IP economics working paper.

### Tier 4: Trade Reporting (for deal flow and current developments)
- **Deadline** — deal announcements, hiring, industry moves.
- **Variety** — industry analysis, data reporting, VIP deep dives on business models.
- **The Hollywood Reporter** — executive moves, platform strategy.
- **The Wrap** — creator economy coverage specifically.
- **Screen International** — international dimension of content trade.
- **Puck** — insider power dynamics across Hollywood, Silicon Valley, Wall Street.

### Tier 5: Creator Economy Specialized
- **Like & Subscribe News** (Substack) — creator economy deal tracking and trend analysis.
- **Streaming Media** — platform strategy and technology.
- **NAB Amplify** — podcast-to-video pipeline analysis.

## Research Agent Operating Parameters

### When investigating a topic:
1. Always search Tier 1 analysts first — Ball, Rushfield, Belloni may have already written the definitive piece.
2. Ground claims in Tier 2 data where possible — audience numbers, demand metrics, viewership data.
3. Use academic sources for structural frameworks, not for current developments.
4. Trade reporting for deal flow and current state, but always push beyond reporting to implication.
5. Every finding should connect back to one of the three thesis forces (supply exhaustion, demand migration, discovery bridge).

### Self-improvement criteria:
- After each cycle, assess whether findings are clustered in one area of the framework or distributed across it.
- Prioritize gaps in the demand-side argument (audience migration, demo analysis, conversion data) — this is currently the thinnest area.
- Track contradictions between what platforms say publicly and what the data shows — these are bullshit flags for the thesis, just as they are for The Brief.
